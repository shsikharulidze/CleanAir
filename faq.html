Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about secondhand smoke, quitting tobacco, and clean indoor air policies

Yes, secondhand smoke is extremely dangerous. The CDC states there is no safe level of exposure to secondhand smoke. Even brief exposure can cause immediate harm to the heart and blood vessels, and increase the risk of heart attack and stroke. Long-term exposure significantly increases the risk of heart disease, stroke, and lung cancer in non-smokers.

Key fact: Secondhand smoke causes about 41,000 deaths among non-smoking adults each year in the United States.

Secondhand smoke can linger in indoor air for hours, even with windows open or fans running. The smallest particles can remain airborne for hours and settle on surfaces, creating what's called "thirdhand smoke" that continues to be harmful.

Fine particles from tobacco smoke can:

  • Stay airborne for 2-3 hours in still air
  • Penetrate walls, floors, and ceilings
  • Stick to furniture, carpets, and clothing
  • Re-enter the air when disturbed

Both smoking and vaping indoors pose health risks to others. While vaping produces aerosol instead of smoke, this aerosol still contains harmful chemicals including nicotine, heavy metals, and other toxic substances.

Important: Most clean indoor air laws apply to both smoking and vaping. The aerosol from e-cigarettes is not harmless water vapor - it contains chemicals that can harm bystanders, especially children and people with respiratory conditions.

Here are some respectful ways to address the situation:

  • "I have a sensitivity to smoke - would you mind moving to a designated smoking area?"
  • "There's a smoke-free policy here that applies to this area."
  • "I'd appreciate it if you could smoke away from the entrance where others need to pass."
  • "Could you help keep this area smoke-free for everyone's health?"

If someone is smoking in a prohibited area, you can also report it to the appropriate authorities or building management.

Weight gain is a common concern, but it's manageable and the health benefits of quitting far outweigh this risk.

The facts:

  • Average weight gain is 5-10 pounds
  • Not everyone gains weight when quitting
  • Weight gain often levels off after the first year
  • The health benefits of quitting are much greater than the risks of modest weight gain

Tips to minimize weight gain:

  • Stay hydrated and eat regular, healthy meals
  • Keep healthy snacks available for when you have cravings
  • Increase physical activity
  • Talk to your healthcare provider about your concerns

Most people try to quit several times before succeeding permanently. This is completely normal and doesn't mean you're weak or that you'll never succeed.

The statistics:

  • Most successful quitters tried 6-7 times before succeeding
  • Each quit attempt is practice for the next one
  • You learn what works and what doesn't with each attempt
  • Support from quitlines can double your success rate

Remember: Every quit attempt is a step toward success, not a failure. The important thing is to keep trying and use the resources available to help you.

No. There are no safe cigarettes. "Light," "low-tar," "natural," or "organic" cigarettes are not safer than regular cigarettes.

Why "light" cigarettes aren't safer:

  • They contain the same harmful chemicals
  • People often smoke them differently (inhaling deeper, smoking more)
  • They still cause cancer, heart disease, and other serious health problems
  • The only safe level of smoking is zero

The tobacco industry has marketed these products as safer, but decades of research show they are just as dangerous as regular cigarettes.

If you encounter someone smoking in a smoke-free area, here are your options:

Immediate steps:

  • Politely inform them about the no-smoking policy
  • Point to posted no-smoking signs if available
  • Suggest designated smoking areas if they exist
  • Move away to protect yourself from exposure

If the behavior continues:

  • Report to building management or security
  • Contact campus safety or administration
  • Report to local health authorities if it's a persistent problem
  • Document the incident with time, date, and location

Remember, you have the right to clean, smoke-free air in public spaces.

The health benefits of quitting begin almost immediately:

Timeline of benefits:

  • 20 minutes: Heart rate and blood pressure drop
  • 12 hours: Carbon monoxide level drops to normal
  • 2 weeks to 3 months: Circulation improves and lung function increases
  • 1 to 12 months: Coughing and shortness of breath decrease
  • 1 year: Risk of heart disease is half that of a smoker
  • 5 years: Risk of stroke is the same as a non-smoker
  • 10 years: Risk of lung cancer is half that of a smoker

These benefits start regardless of how long you've been smoking or how much you smoke.

Yes, quitlines are completely free and confidential. You don't need insurance or a referral to use them.

What quitlines provide:

  • Free telephone counseling with trained quit coaches
  • Personalized quit plans
  • Follow-up support calls
  • Free or discounted nicotine replacement therapy (in many states)
  • Online tools and resources
  • Text message support programs

Privacy: Your information is kept strictly confidential and is not shared without your permission. You can remain anonymous if you prefer.

Languages: Most quitlines offer support in multiple languages, including Spanish.
